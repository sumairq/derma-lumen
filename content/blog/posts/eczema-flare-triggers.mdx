If you have eczema, you've probably been told to avoid almost everything: dairy, gluten, dust, stress, wool, fragrance, soap. Some of these triggers are real. Some are exaggerated. Most are highly individual.

Here's what we tell patients in clinic.

## Triggers that are usually real

- **Heat and sweating** — common, especially in summer
- **Dry air / low humidity** — winter flares are textbook
- **Stress** — well-documented, often delayed by a few days
- **Sodium lauryl sulfate and harsh surfactants** in body wash
- **Wool and rough fabrics** against skin
- **Fragrance** in lotions and detergents

## Triggers that get overhyped

- **Food** (in adults) — adult eczema is rarely meaningfully food-driven. A diet overhaul rarely helps and often causes nutritional gaps.
- **"Toxins"** — there's no scientifically validated cleanse or detox for eczema.
- **Most "anti-inflammatory" supplements** — evidence is weak for the majority.

In children, food allergies do occasionally contribute. Allergy testing is helpful when there are specific reaction patterns. Random elimination diets without evidence usually create stress without benefit.

## What actually controls eczema

The foundation is **barrier repair**: bland, fragrance-free moisturizer applied thickly and often, especially within 3 minutes of getting out of the shower.

On top of that:

- **Topical anti-inflammatories** (steroids, calcineurin inhibitors, ruxolitinib) used proactively in flare-prone areas
- **For moderate-to-severe disease:** modern biologics like Dupixent have transformed eczema care
- **Trigger awareness**, not trigger paranoia

## When to see a dermatologist

If you're using over-the-counter hydrocortisone regularly, or your skin is keeping you awake, see a dermatologist. Modern eczema therapy has changed dramatically in the last five years — there's a very good chance we can do better than what you're doing now.
