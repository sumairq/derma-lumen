Sunscreen is the single highest-leverage product in any skincare routine. It prevents the majority of visible aging, reduces skin cancer risk, and controls every pigmentation condition we treat. The trick is using one you'll actually wear every day.

## The two types

**Mineral sunscreens** (zinc oxide, titanium dioxide) sit on top of the skin and physically scatter UV. They're gentle, safe in pregnancy, and great for sensitive skin and rosacea. The downside: they can leave a white cast, especially on deeper skin tones.

**Chemical sunscreens** absorb UV and convert it to heat. They're typically lightweight, cosmetically elegant, and easier to wear daily. Modern filters used outside the U.S. (Tinosorb, Mexoryl) are excellent — and slowly becoming available in U.S. formulations.

## What SPF actually means

- **SPF 30** blocks ~97% of UVB
- **SPF 50** blocks ~98% of UVB
- **SPF 100** blocks ~99%

The difference between SPF 30 and SPF 50 is small in theory but real in practice — because nobody applies the recommended amount. SPF 30+ is the floor; SPF 50 gives meaningful real-world insurance.

## How much to apply

The studies use **2 mg/cm²** — which works out to roughly:

- A nickel-sized dollop for the face
- A shot glass for full body

Most people use less than half this much. If you want the SPF on the label, apply the amount on the label.

## Daily wear matters more than the label

The best sunscreen is the one you'll wear every morning. If a heavy mineral formula sits in the drawer while your skin gets sun damage, the elegant chemical formula was the better choice all along.

## What about during cosmetic treatments?

After lasers, peels, microneedling, or any procedure that disrupts the skin barrier, sunscreen becomes non-negotiable. New pigmentation in healing skin can be permanent.
