Melanoma is the most dangerous form of skin cancer — but caught early, it's nearly always curable. **The ABCDEs of melanoma** are a simple checklist designed to help you recognize moles that need a closer look.

## A — Asymmetry

Most benign moles are symmetric: you could draw a line through them and the two halves would look alike. Melanomas often look uneven — one side different from the other.

## B — Border

Benign moles tend to have smooth, even borders. Melanomas often have notched, scalloped, or blurred edges.

## C — Color

Watch for moles with **more than one color** — a mix of brown, black, red, white, or blue. Benign moles are typically one color throughout.

## D — Diameter

Most melanomas are larger than 6 mm (about the size of a pencil eraser) when diagnosed — but they can be smaller. Don't dismiss a small mole that meets the other criteria.

## E — Evolving

Any mole that is **changing** — in size, shape, color, or sensation (itching, bleeding) — deserves evaluation. This is the most important letter.

## The "ugly duckling" sign

Trust your instincts. If you have a mole that looks different from your other moles — that stands out as the **odd one out** — have it checked, even if it doesn't tick the ABCDE boxes precisely.

## When to see a dermatologist

- **Yearly screening** is recommended for most adults living in sunny climates like Texas.
- **More often** if you have a personal or family history of skin cancer, many atypical moles, or fair skin with significant sun exposure.
- **Right away** for any rapidly changing mole, a new mole appearing in adulthood, or a lesion that bleeds or doesn't heal.

## A note on self-exams

Once a month, take photos of any moles you're watching. Compare over time. This is one of the highest-value habits you can build for your long-term skin health.
