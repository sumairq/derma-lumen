The acne aisle is overwhelming, and most of what's marketed there will not move the needle on real, persistent acne. The truth is that **acne is several different conditions wearing the same name**, and the right treatment depends entirely on which version of acne you have.

This guide is a dermatologist's framework for choosing — not a list of products to buy.

## Step 1: Identify your acne type

There are four broad patterns we see in clinic:

- **Comedonal acne**: blackheads and whiteheads, usually without much redness. Responds best to topical retinoids and salicylic acid.
- **Inflammatory acne**: red, sore bumps and pustules. Needs anti-inflammatory therapy — usually a combination of topical and oral.
- **Cystic / nodular acne**: deep, painful lumps that take weeks to resolve. Often needs systemic therapy and frequently leaves scars.
- **Hormonal acne**: cyclical breakouts along the jawline and chin. Responds well to spironolactone and combined oral contraceptives in women.

Most patients have a combination. The dominant pattern usually drives the treatment choice.

## Step 2: Match the right tool

Here's a rough framework dermatologists use:

| Acne type | First-line options |
|-----------|--------------------|
| Comedonal | Topical retinoid (tretinoin, adapalene) |
| Mild inflammatory | Topical retinoid + benzoyl peroxide ± topical antibiotic |
| Moderate inflammatory | Above + oral antibiotic or hormonal therapy |
| Severe / cystic / scarring | Isotretinoin (Accutane) |
| Hormonal pattern | Spironolactone or combined oral contraceptive |

## Step 3: Treat aggressively, then taper

The cardinal mistake patients make is **underdosing and overdosing on the wrong things**. Mild prescription routines used for years can plateau. Strong routines used for months can change a life.

The other cardinal mistake is **not treating to prevent scarring**. Scars are forever. Acne is not. If you're scarring, you should be on stronger therapy, not weaker.

## Step 4: Consider in-office adjuncts

Once active acne is controlled, the cosmetic dermatology toolkit takes over:

- **Chemical peels** ([see our chemical peels page](/chemical-peels)) — for texture and post-acne pigmentation
- **Microneedling** ([microneedling overview](/microneedling)) — for atrophic acne scars
- **Laser resurfacing** ([laser resurfacing](/laser-skin-resurfacing)) — for the deepest scarring

## When to see a dermatologist

If over-the-counter products aren't working after 12 weeks, **stop guessing**. A dermatology visit is typically covered by insurance, and the gap between OTC and prescription therapy is enormous.
